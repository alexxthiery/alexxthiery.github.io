Ensemble Kalman Smoother (EnKS)

enkf

Published

19 11 2023

Consider a linear-Gaussian state space model with \(\mathbb{R}^{D_x}\)-valued dynamics \(X_{t+1} \sim F \, X_t + \mathcal{N}(0,Q)\) and \(\mathbb{R}^{D_y}\)-valued observations \(Y_t \sim H X_t + \mathcal{N}(0,R)\). Assuming a Gaussian initial distribution, the filtering distributions \(\mathop{\mathrm{P}}(X_t \in dx \, | Y_{1:t}) \equiv \mathcal{N}(\mu_{t|t}, P_{t|t})\) are Gaussian and can be sequentially computed with the Kalman Filter. Given observations \(y_{1:T}\) and \(1 \leq t \leq T\), the smoothing distributions \(\mathop{\mathrm{P}}(X_t \in dx \, | Y_{1:T}) \equiv \mathcal{N}(\mu_{t|T}, P_{t|T})\) can computed by performing a “backward pass”. Since everything is linear and Gaussian, it is just an exercise in Linear Algebra & Gaussian-conditioning, as described by the Rauch-Tung-Striebel (Rauch, Tung, and Striebel 1965) smoothing recursions. At time \(T\), the filtering and smoothing distributions are identical and the backward recursion reads

\[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} \mu_{t|T} &= \mu_{t|t} + B_t \, {\left( \mu_{t+1|T} - \mu_{t+1|t} \right)} \\ P_{t|T} &= P_{t|t} + B_t {\left( P_{t+1|T} - P_{t+1|t} \right)} B^\top_{t} \end{aligned} \right. \tag{1}\]

for predictive mean \(\mu_{t+1|t} = F \, \mu_{t|t}\) and covariance \(P_{t+1|t} = F \, P_{t|t} \, F^\top + Q\). The smoothing gain matrix is given by

\[ \begin{align} B_t &= \mathop{\mathrm{Cov}}(X_t, X_{t+1} \, | y_{1:t}) \, \mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X_{t+1} \, | y_{1:t})^{-1} \\ &= P_{t|t} F^\top \, {\left( F \, P_{t|t} \, F^\top + Q \right)} ^{-1}. \end{align} \]

Now, we would like to design an “ensemble” version of these updates so that it can be adapted to non-linear dynamics. For this purpose, consider \((X^f_t, X^p_{t+1})\) with \(X^f_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{t|t}, P_{t|t})\) and \(X^p_{t+1} = F \, X^f_t + \mathcal{N}(0,Q)\). In other words, the pair \((X^f_t, X^p_{t+1})\) is obtained by assimilating all the observation \(y_{1:t}\) with a standard Kalman filter; \(X^f_t\) is distributed according to the filtering at time \(t\) while \(X^p_{t+1}\) is distributed to the predictive distribution given \(y_{1:t}\). The standard gaussian conditional probabilities give that

\[ X^f_t | (X^p_{t+1}=x_{t+1}) \; \sim \; \mu_{t|t} + B_T (x_{t+1} - \mu_{t+1|t}) + \mathcal{N}(0, P_{t|t} - B_t \, P_{t+1|t} \, B_t^\top), \]

so that the smoothing gain matrix \(B_t\) can be thought of as being obtained by regressing \(X^f_t\) against \(X^p_{t+1}\), i.e. it is the matrix that minimizes the MSE

\[ \mathop{\mathrm{E}} {\left( \left\| X^f_t - B (X^p_{t+1} - \mu_{t+1|t}) \right\|^2 \right)} . \]

Now, assume that one can add a stochastic correction term \(\Delta_{t+1}\) to \(X^p_{t+1}\) by setting

\[ X^{s}_{t+1} = X^p_{t+1} + \Delta_{t+1} \]

in such a manner that \(X^{s}_{t+1}\) is distributed according to the smoothing distribution, i.e. \(X^{s}_{t+1} = \mathcal{N}(\mu_{t|T}, P_{t|T})\). Let us look at the distribution of the random variable \(X^{s}_{t}\) defined as

\[ \begin{align} X^{s}_{t} &= X^f_t + B_t \, (X^{s}_{t+1} - X^p_{t+1})\\ &= X^f_t + B_t \, \Delta_{t+1}. \end{align} \tag{2}\]

The random variable \(X^{s}_{t}\) is obtained by “pulling” the correction term \(\Delta_{t+1} = X^{s}_{t+1} - X^p_{t+1}\) back to \(X^f_t\) through the “regression” smoothing gain matrix \(B_t\). Writing

\[ X^f_{t} = \mu_{t|t} + B_t \, (X^p_{t+1} - \mu_{t+1|t}) + \varepsilon_t \]

where \(\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, P_{t|t} - B_t \, P_{t+1|t} \, B_t^\top)\) is independent from all other sources of randomness, one immediately obtains that \(X^{s}_{t}\) is Gaussian with mean \(\mu_{t|t} + B_t \, {\left( \mu_{t+1|T} - \mu_{t+1|t} \right)} \) and variance \(P_{t|t} + B_t {\left( P_{t+1|T} - P_{t+1|t} \right)} B^\top_{t}\). Consequently, Equation 1 shows that \(X^{s}_{t}\) is actually distributed according to the smoothing distribution at time \(t\), as we hoped. In other words, Equation 2 describes a mechanism for back-propagating information and recursively construct the smoothing distributions during a backward pass. Furthermore, it is straightforward to adapt the methods to nonlinear scenarios. Run an ensemble Kalman filter (EnKF) with \(N\) particles to obtain particle approximations of the filtering and predictive distributions,

\[ \left\{ \begin{align} \tfrac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \delta(x^{f}_{t,i}) \; &\approx \; \mathop{\mathrm{P}}(X_t \in dx \, | y_{1:t})\\ \tfrac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \delta(x^{p}_{t,i}) \; &\approx \; \mathop{\mathrm{P}}(X_t \in dx \, | y_{1:t-1}).\\ \end{align} \right. \]

These particles can be used to approximate the “regression” smoothing gain matrices

\[ \widehat{B}_t = \mathop{\mathrm{Cov}} {\left( x^f_{t,i}, x^p_{t+1,i} \right)} \, \mathop{\mathrm{Var}} {\left( x^p_{t+1,i} \right)} ^{-1}. \]

Finally, the smoothing distributions can be approximating during a backward pass by setting

\[ x^{s}_{t,i} \; = \; \widehat{B}_t {\left( x^{s}_{t+1,i} - x^{p}_{t+1,i} \right)} . \]
